Dear friends of mathematics,

In 1979, when I was seventeen, the mathematician **Koos Verhoeff** became my
mentor. Among the projects we built together was a sphere-packing puzzle on
the face-centered cubic lattice: **25 distinct pieces, each four spheres**,
assembling into shapes of a hundred spheres and more. We never exhausted it —
nobody could. Computed exactly from the piece data, the classic 100-sphere
shape admits **nearly 10⁹³ possible assembly sequences** — over a trillion
times the number of atoms in the observable universe. One target shape, the
Hollow Pyramid, carried a 1,000-guilder reward for years before two brothers
finally cracked it with solvers of their own.

Koos passed in 2018. This puzzle is part of how I keep his thinking alive —
and today it lives again, as a free app anyone can play in a browser:

**I've recorded my own solve of one shape. When you open the link below, my
run replays against you, piece by piece, in real time. Beat me and the dare
becomes yours to send on.**

**→ [Race me: koospuzzle.com/c/XXXXX]** *(replace with fresh challenge link + ?via=momath)*

A few things the mathematically curious will enjoy:

- Most shapes in the gallery have **never been solved by a human**. The first
  solver of a shape holds that on record, permanently.
- You can **design your own shapes** in the lattice and dare anyone to solve
  them.
- An AI archive, **Ask Anton**, answers questions about the puzzle, the
  mathematics, and forty years of the Verhoeff collaboration — including
  Koos's connection to M.C. Escher.
- There is a **physical prototype** (red, white, and stainless steel). If
  enough people want one, I will manufacture it — the app is where I'm
  listening.

No account is needed to play. It runs on your phone or desktop, in twelve
languages, free.

I'll be at **Bridges Galway this week** with the physical prototype — come
find me, or come find my ghost in the app.

— Anton Bakker

*P.S. — The puzzle is one of the hardest ever devised; the 16-sphere starter
is not. Begin there: [koospuzzle.com]. Sixty seconds and you'll understand
the lattice.*
